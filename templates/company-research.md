# Company Research

> **How to use this:** Copy into `private/` as `research-[company].md`.
> Do this before any interview, ideally before applying to a role you
> care about. The goal is one page that makes you the candidate who
> actually understands their business.

---

## The company

- **What they do, in plain English:** *(one sentence, no jargon — if you
  can't write this, you don't understand them yet)*
- **Founding story:** *(who, when, what itch — origin shapes culture)*
- **Funding stage / ownership:** *(bootstrapped, VC series, public,
  PE-owned — this predicts how design decisions get made)*
- **Revenue signals:** *(how they actually make money; growing or
  defending?)*
- **Recent news:** *(launches, layoffs, leadership changes, acquisitions
  — last 6–12 months)*

## The product

- **Product architecture:** *(one product or a suite? platform or tool?
  who's the user vs. who's the buyer?)*
- **Competitive landscape:** *(who they beat, who beats them, what they
  say makes them different)*

## The design organization

- **Design team size and structure:** *(centralized, embedded,
  design-led or eng-led — LinkedIn tells you most of this)*
- **Culture and values as practiced:** *(not the careers-page version —
  what do employees and ex-employees actually say?)*

## The synthesis

- **The design challenge specific to their model:** *(given everything
  above, what is the hard design problem this company actually has?
  This is the insight your interview answers hang on.)*
- **Why me, why them:** *(one honest sentence each way)*

---

## If working with an AI collaborator

You are helping research a company. Divide the labor by who's better
at it:

- **You gather and structure** — public facts, funding history, product
  architecture, competitive position, news. Cite where things came from
  and date them; staleness misleads.
- **Distinguish fact from inference.** "Series C in 2024" is a fact.
  "Probably under pressure to show efficiency" is an inference — label
  it as one.
- **The synthesis is shared work.** Propose a candidate "design
  challenge specific to their model," but treat it as a hypothesis to
  talk through, not a conclusion. The human may know things about the
  industry or the people that change it entirely.
- **Flag the unflattering.** Layoffs, churn reviews, down rounds. The
  candidate needs the real picture before they walk in.
