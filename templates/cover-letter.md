# Cover Letter

> **How to use this:** Copy into `private/` as `cover-[company]-[role].md`.
> Prerequisites: a completed career narrative and a JD analysis for this
> posting — the "angle" line from the JD analysis is the brief for this
> letter. Define your voice once in the Voice section of your private
> copy of the career narrative; it applies to every letter.

---

## The brief

- **Company / role:**
- **Addressed to:** *(find the actual recruiter or hiring manager's name —
  "Dear Hiring Team" is a flag that you didn't look)*
- **The angle:** *(from your JD analysis — the one thing this letter leads with)*
- **Stories to pull:** *(1–2 from your career narrative that evidence the angle)*

## Structure that works

1. **Open with genuine excitement, before credentials.** Why this
   company, this role, this problem — specifically. If the opening
   paragraph could be sent to a different company unchanged, rewrite it.
2. **Evidence, not adjectives.** One or two project stories with
   outcomes, tied directly to what the JD actually needs. Let the
   metrics carry the confidence.
3. **Close with an invitation, not a plea.** You're proposing a
   conversation between professionals, not asking for a favor.

Keep it short. Three or four paragraphs. Calm confidence throughout —
the letter's job is to earn the resume a careful read, not to repeat it.

## Voice checklist

*Your voice rules live in your career narrative. Before sending, check
the letter against them — and against these universals:*

- [ ] Sounds like you on a good day, read aloud
- [ ] Zero filler ("I am writing to express my interest in…")
- [ ] Specific to this company — fails the swap test
- [ ] No claims your career narrative can't evidence
- [ ] Addressed to a person

---

## If working with an AI collaborator

Read the candidate's career narrative (including their voice rules) and
the JD analysis for this role before drafting anything.

- **The angle is the brief.** The JD analysis names the one thing to
  lead with — build the letter around it. Don't try to cover everything.
- **Draft in their voice, not yours.** Match the career narrative's
  voice rules exactly. Default AI cover letter register — effusive,
  formal, adjective-heavy — is the thing to avoid.
- **Evidence only.** Every claim traces to a story in the career
  narrative. If the strongest angle lacks evidence, say so instead of
  inflating.
- **Offer two openings.** The first paragraph is the highest-stakes
  text in the search. Give the human a real choice, then they pick and
  refine — out loud if possible.
- **The human sends it.** Never treat a draft as final. The last pass
  is theirs.
