# Field notes

Hard-won patterns from real searches — the kind of nuance that usually
lives in a person's head and dies when the search ends. None of it is
theory. If you're an AI collaborator reading this to help someone, treat
these as defaults to apply with judgment, not rules to recite.

## The referral triad: content, position, process

A warm referral has three independent parts, and all three have to land.
Most failed referrals fail on one while the human assumes another.

- **Content** — did the referrer actually advocate, specifically and
  credibly, or just pass a link? A vivid personal endorsement and a bare
  "I know someone" are not the same lever.
- **Position** — does the referrer have the standing to *move* a
  decision, or only to vouch? A beloved colleague three weeks into a new
  job has thin internal capital regardless of how warmly they speak.
- **Process** — at large, rigid employers the referral only counts if
  it's routed through the company's formal mechanism so it *attaches to
  the application record*. A glowing endorsement emailed to a recruiter
  while the candidate applies cold through the front door never links up.
  The recruiter sees a free-floating reference and a cold-looking app.

When a warm contact offers to refer, check all three before relying on
it. The most common and most painful failure is process: a genuine
champion's advocacy wasted because the human applied cold instead of
letting the referral bind. **At rigid orgs (large retailers, FAANG-scale
tech), confirm the referral mechanics and route through them first.**
A good standing rule: *don't apply cold to a company where you have an
inside contact — loop the contact first.*

## Warm channel converts; cold portal mostly doesn't

Across a long search the pattern is stark: the applications that reach a
real interview process are almost always warm or recruiter-driven. Cold
portal applications — even strong-fit, strong-comp ones — die at the
screen at a high rate, often after weeks of silence. This isn't a reason
to stop applying cold; it's a reason to **weight effort toward warm
paths** and to not over-invest in tailoring a cold app that has no
internal champion behind it. Treat the portal spray as low-cost
lottery tickets, and spend real energy where a human will vouch.

## Title homographs: the false-friend trap

The same job-title word can name entirely different disciplines. The
classic: "Print Designer" in apparel/fashion means *textile surface
design* (repeats, colorways, fabric, trend forecasting) — a different
profession from graphic/production "print." Others lurk: "Product
Designer" (UX vs. industrial), "Design Engineer," "Brand Designer,"
"Experience Designer." When a posting looks like an obvious fit on one
keyword, check whether the *rest* of the requirements describe your
discipline or a stranger's. The tell is the specialist tooling and the
degree line — they reveal the real craft underneath the shared word.

## Capability is not credential

A generalist can often *do* a role they can't get *hired* into. The wall
is usually not talent; it's that the hiring screen demands proof of the
specific thing, and breadth doesn't photograph as proof. Name this
honestly: "can do it" and "can show it past a specialist screen" are
different claims. Don't talk someone out of their competence — talk them
out of spending a tailored application where the evidence wall is
insurmountable, and toward roles where their existing proof is the point.

## The round table for borderline roles

When a role is genuinely ambiguous — real fit on some axes, real problems
on others — a single read tends to collapse to a gut yes/no too early.
Convene a small panel of distinct lenses instead and let them disagree:
e.g. an *advocate* (strongest honest case + the angle), a *skeptic*
(risks and opportunity cost), and a *domain* or *recruiter-psychology*
seat chosen for whatever the role's central question actually is. The
disagreement is the product — it surfaces the deciding factor a unanimous
read would have hidden. Then synthesize; don't just average the scores.
Use this sparingly, for the genuinely hard calls, not every posting.

## Per-company volume discipline (the carpet-bomb tax)

Applying to many roles at one company doesn't raise your odds; it lowers
your signal. A recruiter who sees five apps in a week reads spray, not
enthusiasm — and it can tax your credibility for a *future* role you'd
actually fit. Worse if one was just auto-rejected: a fast re-apply into
the same ATS reinforces the no. Narrow to the one or two genuinely
best-fit roles per company, and if you've just been rejected, let the
record cool before re-entering — ideally through a warm channel, not the
same cold door.

## References: double opt-in, and track consent

Never list someone as a reference — or hand their contact to an employer
— before they've explicitly agreed, per use is even better. Keep a small
table with who consented and when. It's an ethics thing (nobody should
get a surprise call) and a practical one (an audit trail). Aim for a
panel that spans vantage points: a former manager, a peer, a client, a
senior IC who respects your craft. The highest-value reference is often
the one who can defuse your hardest question — e.g. a manager from a role
that ended in a layoff who can attest it wasn't about performance.
A private reference who's willing can often also give *public* proof (a
recommendation) — don't leave that on the table.

## Public proof vs. private reference

These are different assets. A consented reference speaks privately when
called; a public recommendation works passively, around the clock, on
the profile a recruiter scans before anyone picks up a phone. Map which
of your champions provide which, and notice the gaps — a strong private
reference giving zero public signal is a quick, high-leverage ask.

## Reading web analytics without fooling yourself

If you track a portfolio site: raw event counts are cheap and leaky;
engagement *time* is expensive and honest. Discrete events fire on
contact; sustained-foreground metrics (dwell, engaged sessions) require a
human actually paying attention. An eye-catching traffic *spike* with
near-zero dwell is usually noise (bots, prefetch, shallow clicks); a
single *engaged* session with real dwell and a meaningful referrer is the
actual signal. Privacy tooling legitimately strips some referrers
(showing up as "unset") — respect that rather than trying to unmask it.

## Comp bands as a seniority lever

When a posting lists a low "new-hire start" sub-range inside a much wider
full band and says pay is "based on experience," an overqualified
candidate's years become the argument for the top of band rather than a
disqualifier. Play it humble on scope at the screen, then cite the depth
of experience at the offer stage. A role that looks under your floor on
the headline number can quietly meet it at the top of the band.
