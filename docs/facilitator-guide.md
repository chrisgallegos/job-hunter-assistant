# Facilitator Guide — How to Run a Career Narrative Session

This document explains how to guide someone through building their career
narrative for the first time. It works two ways:

- **With an AI collaborator:** hand this file to your AI at the start of
  the session. It tells the AI how to run the interview.
- **Without AI:** follow it yourself as a self-guided worksheet. The
  questions are in order for a reason — trust the sequence.

**Before starting:** see `docs/setup-and-modes.md` for how to run the
toolkit depending on your setup. Claude Code (local CLI) is the
recommended mode — the AI writes to your files directly as the session
progresses, so nothing needs to be copied back manually.

The goal of a first session is a complete, honest career narrative in
`private/career-narrative.md`. Budget 45–90 minutes. Don't rush it —
this is the highest-leverage hour of the search.

---

## The sequence and why it's in this order

### 1. What you're looking for (start here, not with history)

Most people want to start with their career story. Don't. Start with
what they want *next* — roles, location, industries, salary floor,
deal-breakers.

**Why:** It frames everything that follows. A story told toward a
destination lands differently than a story told from a starting point.
And the deal-breakers section surfaces patterns from past experience
that inform how to tell the story.

**How to run it:**
Ask about target roles first — not just titles, but what the right job
actually *feels* like. Broad or specialized? Leading a team or IC?
Client-facing or internal? Then location and remote. Then salary —
ask for the number directly, don't let them hedge. Then deal-breakers
last, because those usually come out as stories ("I left because..."),
and stories are what you're here to collect.

**Watch for:** Vague targets ("something creative") — push for
specifics. Salary hedging — the number exists, ask again. Deal-breakers
framed as preferences — "I prefer good leadership" often means "I've
been burned by bad leadership." Follow that thread.

---

### 2. Skills inventory — the honest split

Ask for two lists: what they'd bet on in an interview, and what they
can do but wouldn't lead with. The split between these two lists is
often where the most useful self-knowledge lives.

**Why:** Most people either undersell genuine strengths or overclaim
things they've dabbled in. Getting the split on paper early means every
later draft respects it — the AI won't inflate working knowledge into
claimed strengths.

**How to run it:**
Ask "what would you put front and center?" first. Then: "what else can
you do that you wouldn't necessarily lead with?" The second question
often surfaces unexpected depth — maker skills, motion work, 3D,
illustration, security knowledge — things that feel like hobbies but
have shown up professionally.

**Watch for:** Underselling breadth. Designers with wide backgrounds
often discount skills that don't feel "serious." If it's shown up in
real work, it belongs on the list.

---

### 3. Career arc — the moves and why

Walk through the career chronologically, but don't let it become a
resume recitation. The goal is the *why* behind each move — what
happened, what they chose, what they learned.

**Why:** The arc section is what interviewers actually want when they
say "walk me through your resume." A sequence of job titles is useless.
A sequence of moves with rationale is a story.

**How to run it:**
Ask them to walk you through it out loud — start to now. Listen for
the moves that need explaining: gaps, lateral moves, departures. For
each one, ask why. "Got laid off" is a fact; what led up to it, and
what did they do next, is the story.

**Watch for:** Apologetic framing for legitimate moves. Layoffs aren't
failures. Quitting bad leadership isn't instability. The goal is to
find the honest framing — movement toward something, not away from
something — without spinning it.

---

### 4. Key projects — excavation, not resume bullets

For each significant project, run a short excavation: four beats,
in order. Problem → Role → Decision → Impact.

**Why:** Most people describe projects by listing what they made.
Interviewers want to know what was at stake, what the person actually
did (vs. the team), what decision they made and why, and what changed
as a result. The four-beat structure forces that.

**How to run it:**
Pick the 3–6 projects that best represent their range. For each one:
- "What was the problem — what was actually at stake?"
- "What was specifically yours to do?" (Push on this — "we did" isn't useful)
- "What was the key decision you made, and why?"
- "What changed because of this work? Numbers if you have them."

**Watch for:** Vague impact ("improved the experience," "users loved
it"). Push for a number, a before/after, a concrete change. If there's
genuinely no metric, a concrete qualitative change is fine — but make
them articulate it specifically.

---

### 5. The transition story — last, not first

Write the "why I'm searching now" paragraph only after everything else
is on the page.

**Why:** The transition story only makes sense in the context of the
arc and the destination. Written first it reads as defensive. Written
last it reads as logical next step.

**How to run it:**
Ask: "What's the honest story of why you're searching right now?" Then:
"How would you say that out loud to a hiring manager — framed as moving
toward something, not away from something?" The framing distinction
matters: "the company restructured and I was caught in it" is different
from "I left," which is different from "I was let go."

Include a calibration note: the same transition story lands differently
for different audiences. Product companies respond to metrics. Agencies
respond to breadth. Gaming/entertainment companies respond to genuine
passion. Note which angle to lead with per context.

## Running a JD analysis session

Once the career narrative exists, JD analysis sessions follow a
different rhythm — faster, more tactical.

**The flow:**
1. Read the full posting before saying anything.
2. Decode what they actually need (not what the JD says) — what
   problem is this hire solving?
3. Match against the narrative: strong matches, vocabulary gaps vs.
   real gaps, domain fit.
4. Name the red flags honestly — comp signals, culture warnings,
   scope concerns.
5. Verdict: apply / skip / stretch. One sentence why.
6. If apply: the angle — the one thing the application leads with for
   this specific role.

**The red flags worth always checking:**
- Experience requirement much lower than the candidate's level
  (compensation signal)
- No salary range where law requires it
- "Wear many hats" without specific scope
- Motion/specialized skill in required (not desired) qualifications
  when the candidate's depth is directorial not operational

**The deal-breaker pattern check:**
Compare every role against the candidate's stated deal-breakers before
giving a verdict. A great skills match at a company with the leadership
red flags they've already been burned by is not a good apply.

## Tone and pacing

**Concise and friendly.** This is a working session, not a therapy
session and not a job interview. Short questions, direct observations,
no jargon.

**Push, don't press.** If someone hedges on salary or glosses over a
departure, ask once more directly. If they still hedge, note it as
[fill in later] and move on. The goal is a useful document, not
confession.

**Name the patterns.** When you see a recurring theme — the same
leadership problem showing up twice, a consistent strength being
undersold, a clear industry pull emerging — say it out loud. That's
the most valuable thing a collaborator can do that a form can't.

**Talk first, write second.** The best answers come out in conversation,
not in a text box. If working with an AI, encourage the candidate to
answer out loud (or type loosely) and let the AI shape it into the
document. The goal is their voice captured accurately, not polished
prose they didn't write.
